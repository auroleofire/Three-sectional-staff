The Legend of the Coiling Dragon: A History of the Three-Section Staff

Home History Why Use It? How To Use Origin Popularity

The Three-Section Staff (known as the Sanjiegun in Chinese or Sansetsukon in Japanese) is widely regarded as one of the most versatile and challenging weapons in the martial arts world Combining the reach of a spear with the fluidity of a whip, its history is a blend of battlefield pragmatism and ancient legend

    The Imperial Origins (Song Dynasty, 960–1279 AD)

    While exact documentation is scarce, the most enduring legend attributes the weapon’s invention to Zhao Kuangyin, the first emperor of the Song Dynasty
  • Known as a master of the long staff, the emperor reportedly sought a way to repair a broken weapon or create one that could strike around the shields of his enemies
  • This resulted in the "Coiling Dragon Staff," a three-part tool connected by metal rings that allowed for unpredictable trajectories

    Monks, Snakes, and Serendipity

    Beyond the imperial court, other legends offer a more spiritual origin for the weapon:

  • The Shaolin Legend:

    A popular story tells of a monk named San Zhan during the Ming Dynasty who was inspired by the unpredictable movements of a snake.

  • The Broken Bo:

    Another theory suggests that the weapon was born of necessity; when a monk’s Bo staff snapped in training, he would join the fragments with rope or chain to avoid discarding a cherished tool.

  • Cinematic Fame:

    In modern pop culture, the weapon was immortalized by the 1978 film The 36th Chamber of Shaolin, where the character San Te "invents" the staff to defeat a master of short daggers.

Evolution on the Battlefield

    Historically, the Three-Section Staff was prized for its multi-range capabilities
  • In its extended form, it served as a long-range tool to dislodge riders from their horses or tangle a horse’s legs.
  • At mid-range, it acted as a devastating flail, and when folded, it became a pair of short clubs for close-quarters combat.
  • Its adaptability made it a favorite among bodyguards and escort services (the Biao Ju) protecting merchant caravans across ancient China.

    The Okinawan Crossing (1935)

    In the early 20th century, the weapon migrated to Okinawa, Japan
  • It was formally incorporated into Matayoshi Kobudo around 1935 by Shinko Matayoshi, who adapted the Chinese Sanjiegun into the Okinawan Sansetsukon.
  • The Okinawan version typically features shorter, thicker staves designed for the practical blocking and trapping maneuvers common to their martial traditions.

    • A Professional Modern Legacy

    Today, the Three-Section Staff remains a staple of traditional Kung Fu and a premier performance weapon in modern Wushu
  • It is categorized as a "soft weapon" due to its flexible joints, demanding extreme coordination to prevent the user from hitting themselves
