The "Grind-and-Bind" Revolution: How the 1970s Redefined the Legend of Hong Kong Action

The "Molting Stage" of a Cultural Icon:


To the casual observer, the 1970s in Hong Kong cinema appear merely as a disorganized precursor to the global "Golden Age" of the 1980s. However, as a cultural strategist, I contend that this decade was a vital "molting phase"—a pupa state of intense, agonizing transformation. It was a period of systemic collapse and radical rebirth, where the industry did not merely "slump," but underwent a structural realignment in response to a society reeling from the 1967 riots.
The post-riot era was one of deep-seated social panic. The colonial government was forced to pivot from distant administration to active "output," attempting to stabilize a predominately Chinese population through populist measures. Simultaneously, the film industry faced a systemic collapse of the traditional block-booking infrastructure as Southeast Asian markets restricted Chinese-language imports. Cantonese cinema, the traditional heartbeat of the local identity, faced an existential threat, eclipsed by the rapid rise of free television. Yet, within this treacherous water, a new cinematic identity was forged—not through a seamless transition, but through the "History, Origin, Benefits, and Use" of a new cultural strategy born of friction.

The Origin: The "Grind-and-Bind" Philosophy (Mo He)


The evolution of Hong Kong’s identity is often mischaracterized by the Western "melting pot" metaphor, which implies a harmonious blending of elements. A more rigorous historical analysis suggests the concept of mo he, or "grind-and-bind." Unlike the melting pot, "grinding" denotes painful contact, friction, and the heat of competition.
In the 1970s, this creative explosion was fueled by the "North versus South" cultural friction. The "North," represented by the Shanghainese-led studio discipline of Shaw Brothers and the Mandarin dialect, provided technical rigor and capital. The "South," rooted in Cantonese opera and local martial arts traditions, provided the indigenous soul. The mo he process ground these disparate forces together—East against West, traditional values against free-market realities—to "bind" a uniquely resilient indigenous identity. It was this painful negotiation, rather than simple assimilation, that birthed the "Hong Kong Legend."

The History: How Television Accidentally Resuscitated the Silver Screen


The 1970s media landscape was defined by a paradoxical cannibalization: free television (TVB and RTV) nearly annihilated Cantonese cinema by stealing its talent and audience, only to provide the very tools for its resuscitation. This was not a linear recovery but a strategic "twists-and-turns" development.
Following the 1973 McKinsey Report, which revolutionized civil service "output" to meet public demands, the government "massified" the audience through housing and education reforms. The shift from the prison-like minimalism of the Shek Kip Mei Estate to the "brand-building" healthiness of the Oi Man Estate created a unified "tribe" of viewers with shared social experiences.
The industry’s counter-intuitive turning point arrived in 1973 when Shaw Brothers director Chor Yuen utilized the skeletal structure of a Shanghainese drama and fleshed it out with the exaggerated, sketch-style farce of TVB’s variety shows. The resulting film, The House of 72 Tenants, utilized TV stars to reinstate the Cantonese spirit on the silver screen. By borrowing the medium that had "destroyed" it, the film industry found a new, high-velocity language that resonated with this newly unified mass audience.

The Benefits: Moving Beyond "Preachy" Moralism to Sensory Excitement


As the decade matured, film aesthetics underwent a strategic shift from the conservative, "preachy" moralism of 1960s wenyi films toward intense sensory excitement. This was a calculated response to the new media environment: because television was strictly censored for "family viewing," cinema was forced to differentiate itself through action, violence, and erotica.
The "benefit" of this transition was the forced adoption of sophisticated "Western" cinematic techniques. To sustain audience attention in an era of rapid stimulus, filmmakers moved away from theatrical narrative modes and embraced montage, complex mise-en-scène, and aggressive pacing. As historian Law Kar observed regarding this shift in moral function:
"I believed that the proliferation of television had led to the decline of moralising and rise in sensory excitement."
By abandoning blunt moral lessons for visceral thrills, Hong Kong cinema developed the technical muscle that would later conquer international markets.

The "Use" of Martial Wisdom: Choreography as the Ultimate Hybrid Art


The technical "Use" of martial arts on screen in the 1970s moved away from the realistic preaching influenced by the 1940s Western realism (the May 4th Movement) and toward a "heightened reality." This was the era of "Chinese learning as essence, Western learning as application"—a strategic variation where traditional skills were repurposed for a modern, global market.
The "Use" involved the operative application of Peking Opera acrobatics (Northern kicks) to revitalize Southern folk heroes. This is epitomized by Jackie Chan’s portrayal of Wong Fei-hung in Drunken Master (1978). By applying Northern-style stage movements to a Southern kung fu legend, and refining the results through Western editing and rhythmic sound effects, the industry created a globally watchable sensation. Sam Ho, paraphrasing Picasso to explain the logic of this "show versus substance" choreography, noted:
"Art is a lie that tells the truth... In films, kung fu that looks good is more important than kung fu that fights good."
This "lie" was a sophisticated aesthetic strategy that achieved a level of visual harmony Western action cinema simply could not replicate.

The 1970s served as a critical "History" of professional martial artists, such as Sammo Hung and Lau Kar-wing, transitioning into the role of directors. These masters utilized their operative backgrounds to "bind" the traditional code of martial ethics (wu de) with the "Resourceful Hero" archetype.
This hero was a strategic reflection of the 1970s Hong Konger: an individual who survived not through raw power or high-status lineage, but through "gags," wit, and adaptability in a shifting, high-pressure economy. Sammo Hung, in his molting stage at the Hong Kong-China Opera Institute, began experimenting with loosely structured scripts and slapstick gags in films like The Iron-Fisted Monk (1977). This was not "slapdash" filmmaking; it was the birth of a new brand of action-comedy that combined traditional virtue with the pragmatic survivalism of a newly minted metropolitan "tribe."

Conclusion: A Forward-Looking Legacy


The 1970s were a rollercoaster of "twists and turns," a decade where struggle was the primary creative engine. This period paved the way for the "touch-and-gold" era of the 1980s, where the unified identity and technical sophistication ground out in the 70s finally reached fruition.
Looking back, we find a profound "depth in the shallow." Even the "slapdash" or "lousy" fight flicks of the 1970s possessed a spiritual and aesthetic harmony—a complementary movement between combatants—that Western genres, such as the cowboy movie, lacked. If the 1970s were about creating a unified identity through the "grind" of North and South, East and West, we must ask: what is the new "grind" facing cinema today? The legacy of the 70s teaches us that it is only through the heat of friction and the willingness to molt that a culture truly finds its wings.
